War and Imperial Defence

The Holy Roman Empire was not a single, unified state and thus differed from most countries as we know them today. Larger and centralised nation states in Europe only emerged in the 19th century while the early modern period was marked by smaller territories that had their own rulers, religious orientation, and legal systems. However, lands belonging to the Holy Roman Empire collaborated when this territorial system was threatened by strong military players from either outside or within. Under the banner of the Imperial Circles, regional defence was organised to support the common effort, and taxes were collected to finance the necessary armies. As opposed to other regions at the time, and in contrast to most modern nation states, the Holy Roman Empire did not have a standing imperial army but levied toops temporarily. This countered fears of excessive imperial power but also caused special logistic challenges in times of war. A war led under the Emperor and locally supported by the Imperial Circles was known as a Reichskrieg (Imperial War) and only declared in the severest circumstances.
