Welcome to the Imperial Circles of the Holy Roman Empire in the early modern century, an epoch in European history that roughly ranges from the 16th to late 18th centuries. This interactive journey invites you to explore how a complex historical structure governed itself across multiple boundaries of jurisdiction, confession and language. The Imperial Circles (Reichskreise) were not abstract administrative units that merely existed on paper: they actively coordinated key functions of the Empire such as military defence, currency regulation, mobility of goods and people, and social issues at a time when no overarching welfare state existed. Their work shaped everyday life from the coins people handled to the soldiers they had to quarter during wars. The image below shows a 1510 woodcut, created by Jost de Negker after a design by Hans Burgkmair the Elder, and visually shows the federal and multi-layered structure of the Holy Roman Empire. Being the Emperor was not a hereditary role but an elective office although most Emperors came from the House of Habsburg. The Emperor was formally elected by seven princely electors, which are highlighted in the woodcut. Other key territories are large duchies like Bavaria, rich and powerful imperial cities like Augsburg and Lübeck, territories governed by lesser nobles (counts and knights), and even free imperial villages and imperial monasteries, a ll of which could claim participation in the Imperial Diets, the Empire’s central body for law-making and negotiation. While the Empire knew clear vertical hierarchies, it was also marked by many horizontal collaborations, in which the Imperial Circles played a special role. To start exploring their most important functions, select one key theme and travel back in time to learn about real historical problems drawn from contemporary sources.
